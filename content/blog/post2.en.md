## Introduction

Supernovae are cataclysmic explosions of massive stars that eject enormous amounts of energy and matter into space. At the UASD Institute of Physics, we have carried out an exhaustive observation campaign that has revealed new details about the evolution and impact of these cosmic events.

## Observations

- **Supernova 2025A**: Detected in the Andean Sky region on March 10. An abrupt increase in brightness followed by a gradual drop was observed.
- **Supernova 2025B**: Located in the star cluster NGC 1234, it showed a neutrino emission signal correlated with the collapse event.
- **Supernova 2025C**: Detected in radio and its interaction with the interstellar medium was studied.

## Methodology

We use large-aperture telescopes located in the Andes mountain range, with high-resolution detection technology and data reduction algorithms. Collaboration with international observatories allowed us to confirm the nature of the supernovae and obtain detailed spectra.

## Impact

- We published 3 articles in high-impact journals on supernova spectroscopy and heavy element production.
- We developed an open-source data processing pipeline, accessible to the scientific community.
- We strengthen local scientific infrastructure and train undergraduate and graduate students in observation and analysis techniques.

## Conclusion

Supernovae remain one of the most fascinating phenomena in the universe. Our work contributes to understanding how the elements that make up everything we know, from water to human bones, are formed. As we expand our observation and analysis capabilities, we will discover more about the life cycle of stars and the cosmos we inhabit.
